Project Summary

This project was designed to determine the effect a city's distance from the equator has on its weather. By randomly generating 500 pairs of latitude and longitude, then using a publicly available tool to find the world city nearest to those coordinates and pulling a snapshot of the weather in that city, we were able to quickly generate a large dataset.

The usefulness of the data is up for debate. Although we were able to confirm a very clear relationship between latitude and temperature, there may have been a slight selection bias towards coastal cities. Due to the Earth's surface being 70% water, any random coordinate is more than twice as likely to end up in water, and thus the closest city to this coordinate will be coastal and often in an extreme location. For example, Ushuaia, Argentina, which is the southernmost city in the world, appears eight times in the list.

A more advanced method of random coordinate selection would be required to avoid issues like this, perhaps one that is aware of where the Earth's large land masses are located.
